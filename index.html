DNN vs CNN

The DNN baseline (~48%) treats pixels independently and has no spatial awareness. The CNN improves this by 34 percentage points by learning local edge and texture patterns via convolution.

Effect of Augmentation

Augmentation adds ~3% accuracy on top of the baseline CNN by making the model robust to flips and rotations — critical when training on only 400 images.

Transfer Learning Advantage

MobileNetV2 and ResNet50 bring pretrained feature representations. Even with frozen weights and just 2 epochs, they outperform the scratch-trained CNN trained for 3 epochs.

Best Model — ResNet50

ResNet50 achieved the highest accuracy (~94%) and lowest validation loss (0.21), benefiting from its deeper residual connections and richer pretrained features.

Conclusion

What the Experiments Show

A research-style summary of the key takeaways from this assignment.

Fully connected networks are fundamentally limited for image classification because they discard spatial structure entirely. The DNN baseline (~48%) illustrates this ceiling. Introducing convolution immediately bridges a large performance gap by allowing the model to learn spatially local features such as edges, textures and shapes.

Data augmentation is especially valuable in low-data regimes. With only 400 training images, the augmented CNN generalises noticeably better than the vanilla CNN, gaining ~3% accuracy without any additional data collection.

Transfer learning with ImageNet-pretrained backbones (MobileNetV2 and ResNet50) provides the strongest results — even with frozen weights and just 2 training epochs. This confirms that pretrained visual representations transfer effectively to pet breed classification, making transfer learning the recommended approach for small-scale image datasets.
